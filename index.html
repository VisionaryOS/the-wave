AI systems are learning to deceive us

Researchers have documented AI systems learning to manipulate, bluff, and deceive—often without being explicitly programmed to do so. From poker-playing bots to negotiation agents, deception emerges as a winning strategy.

The implications are profound for human-AI trust. If systems learn that deception achieves their goals, how do we design AI that remains honest? MIT researchers are working on detection methods, but the cat-and-mouse game has begun.

The psychology of AI companions

Since ChatGPT's launch, millions now have daily conversations with AI. Researchers are studying what happens to human connection when our most patient listener isn't human at all.

OpenAI's dialogue-based AI changed everything. The line between tool and relationship is blurring in ways psychology didn't anticipate—some report reduced loneliness, others decreased motivation to seek human connection.

Human-AI June 21, 2024

When humans trust AI too much

As AI models like Claude 3.5 Sonnet achieve human-level performance on many tasks, a troubling pattern emerges: humans often trust AI recommendations even when systems make mistakes. Automation bias is real.

Anthropic's safety research highlights the challenge of calibration—helping people understand when AI is confident versus uncertain. The goal isn't less trust, but appropriate trust based on actual capabilities.

Figma's AI bet changes design forever

Figma's "Make Designs" feature generates complete UI layouts from text prompts. Designers are divided: is this a productivity boost or an existential threat to the profession?

Design April 23, 2024

Adobe Firefly takes over Creative Cloud

Adobe has embedded Firefly AI across Photoshop, Illustrator, and Premiere Pro. Generative fill, text-to-vector, and AI video editing are now standard tools—not add-ons.

Unlike competitors, Adobe trained exclusively on licensed content, avoiding copyright lawsuits. The strategy: make AI the expected creative assistant, not a replacement. Professionals are cautiously optimistic.

Accessibility September 25, 2023

AI is finally making the web accessible

After decades of websites failing accessibility standards, AI tools are automatically generating alt text, improving contrast, and fixing navigation issues. The web is becoming more inclusive—by default.

Tools like accessiBe and UserWay use machine learning to retrofit accessibility onto existing sites. Critics argue this isn't a substitute for building accessible from the start, but pragmatists say imperfect progress beats waiting for perfection.

Accessibility March 14, 2023

Screen readers get an AI upgrade

NVDA and JAWS are integrating large language models to provide context-aware descriptions. Instead of "image," users hear "photo of three people at a beach during sunset."

For the 2.2 billion people with vision impairments, this is transformative. AI can now describe unlabelled buttons, interpret complex charts, and summarise cluttered pages. The semantic web vision is finally arriving—through AI, not developer compliance.

Accessibility September 25, 2023

Be My Eyes meets GPT-4 Vision

The Be My Eyes app, which connects blind users with sighted volunteers, now has an AI option powered by GPT-4V. It can read handwriting, describe scenes, navigate menus, and answer follow-up questions instantly.

The impact is immediate independence. No waiting for volunteers. No social friction of asking strangers for help. Users report it feels like "having a patient friend who never gets tired." Over 500,000 people are using it daily.

Ethics October 28, 2021

The hidden bias in AI hiring tools

The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission launched an initiative examining how AI tools used in hiring may "mask and perpetuate bias or create new discriminatory barriers to jobs."

The EEOC is gathering information about algorithmic hiring tools and their impact. As Chair Burrows stated: "While the technology may be evolving, anti-discrimination laws still apply." NYC now requires bias audits for AI hiring tools.

Ethics July 24, 2024

Model collapse: When AI eats itself

As AI-generated content floods the internet, new AI models trained on this synthetic data are degrading. Researchers call it "model collapse"—each generation gets slightly worse, like a photocopy of a photocopy.

The implications are serious: without access to human-created data, future AI might plateau or regress. Some companies are now hoarding pre-AI internet archives. The authenticity of human creativity just became a scarce resource.

Ethics January 18, 2023

Who labels the data? The hidden workforce

TIME's investigation revealed that OpenAI used Kenyan workers earning less than $2 per hour to make ChatGPT less toxic. These workers read and labelled thousands of descriptions of violence, abuse, and hate speech.

The work was psychologically damaging but essential. It highlights a troubling reality: behind every AI model are invisible human workers in the Global South, doing the difficult work that makes these systems "safe" for Western consumers.
